Seizure

A seizure is a temporary, abnormal burst of electrical activity in the brain that can cause a person to experience a sudden change in behavior, movement, or consciousness. Seizures can look different from person to person and can include:

  • Uncontrolled shaking
  • Staring into space
  • Loss of consciousness
  • Unusual sensations or thoughts
  • Involuntary twitching or stiffness
  • Severe stiffening and limb shaking
  • A feeling of deja vu
  • Blurred vision
  • Racing thoughts

Most seizures last less than two minutes and stop on their own. However, the person experiencing a seizure can be injured, so it's important to protect them. If you encounter someone having a seizure, you can:

  • Help them to the ground in a safe area
  • Clear the area of sharp objects
  • Loosen any tight clothing
  • Turn the person on their side
  • Stay with the person until they recover or medical help arrives

Some common causes of seizures include high fever, medication, infections, low blood sodium, brain injury, or a tumor. Recurring seizures are known as epilepsy, which can vary in severity and impact from person to person.
